Rationale

Sociolinguistics and literary corpora

  • Scarcity of sociolinguistic studies focused on literary sources (outside of diatopic studies)
  • Importance of describing the linguistic register used in literary writing
  • Literary corpora and exploitation of certain social parameters like gender. Advantages:
    • Known sociologic profile

Research question

What new knowledge concerning genderlects can we gain through statistical methods?

Methodology

Corpus

  • Source: European Literary Text Collection (ELTeC)
  • Exclusion of American authors
  • Corpus: 86 novels (1840–1919)
    • 22 novels by women
    • 7,380,761 words (20% by women)

Zeta

  • Comparison of two groups of texts (G1 and G2)
    • For each group, extraction a number of segments of fixed size
    • For each term (t) in the vocabulary (e.g. lemmas), the segment proportions (ps) of each group in which the term appears at least once (binarization) are calculated
    • Zeta is the result of subtracting the two proportions

  • Software: pyzeta

Results

Distinctive lexicon

  • Zeta will always provide results
  • Comparison of the results obtain with a random sample and with the distinction F/M: significant values of the later

Stereotyped distribution?

Weidman & O’Sullivan (2018) (novels, EN, 1840s–1990s):

  • High frequencies in the female corpus:
    • private/immediate space: home, kitchen, church, hallway, school; door, window, chair, stairs, curtains, garden, room
    • positioning in relation to the body: hair, fingers, skin, eyes, heart, face, cheeks, dress, gown
    • less certainty/confidence: seemed, perhaps, believed, scarcely, likely
    • interaction: laughed, spoke, saying, talking, exclaimed, paused, thank, asked, interrupted
  • High frequencies in the male corpus:
    • broad space: country, earth, city, town, world
    • directional language: east, west, south, north, center, upward, beyond, forth
    • travel: airplane, car, traffic, miles, speed
    • more certainty/confidence: exactly, absolutely, declared, therefore

Distinctive semantic fields

Distinctive lexicon in the female corpus:

  • Family relations: hermano, criatura, niño, marido, madre
  • Body parts: cabello, semblante, seno, vestido
  • Dicendi verbs and interaction language: exclamar, murmurar, notar, responder
  • Nature: flor, sol, pájaro, rama, hoja, rayo, planta, árbol, cielo, tierra, viento, ave
  • Emotion words: felicidad, dichoso, alegre, melancólico, gracioso, sufrir, placer, apacible
  • Adjectives: bello, dulce, lindo, suave, sombrío, severo, oscuro, azul, amargo, delicado, elegante

Distinctive semantic fields

Distinctive lexicon in the male corpus:

  • Spatial words: plaza, calle, dentro, fuera, encima, portal, arriba, balcón
  • Action description: comenzar, ocurrir, inmediato, inmediatamente, acto, resultar, siguiente, continuar, averiguar, resultado
  • Professional domain: noticia, asunto, negocio, cabo, soldado, público, ministro, empresa, espada, capital

Epistemic adverbs and adjectives

Previous studies:

  • Pennebaker (2011: 40): women use more certainty words (always, absolutely)
  • Weidman & O’Sullivan (2018):
    While males throughout all sets have markers distinctly related to language of certainty and confidence—‘exactly’, ‘absolutely’, ‘declared’, and ‘therefore’—female markers remain throughout all sets trending toward language of less confidence, uncertainty (or at least less certainty), and doubt—‘seemed’, ‘perhaps’, ‘believed’, ‘scarcely’, and ‘likely’.

The period signal

  • Previous studies show the preponderance of the period variable (e.g. Stamou 2008; Hoover 2013)
  • In this study, the lexicon of novels from 1840–1859 (T1) in relation to 1900–1919 (T4) is more distinct than the gender partition

The vos / usted distribution

  • Theoretical framework:
    • Principle I: For stable sociolinguistic variables, men use a higher frequency of nonstandard forms than women (Labov 1990: 210)
    • Principle Ia: in change from above, women favor the incoming prestige form more than men (Labov 1990: 213)
  • Hypothesis: women will first use usted and with a higher frequency than vos (cfr. Blayer 2001)

Conclusions

Limitations and future work

  • The results of the automatic annotation need to be improved
  • The relevance of the novel subgenre needs to be evaluated (e.g. historical novel). Cfr. Woolf (1967):
    Had she been born in 1827, Dorothy Osborne would have written novels; had she been born in 1527, she would never have written at all. But she was born in 1627, and [...] there was nothing unseemly in writing a letter
  • Need to isolate direct-speech instances
  • Gender and social analysis of character and narration voices (speech characterisation by social parameters?)

Conclusions

  • Stastical measures can be used to carry out sociolinguistic studies within literary corpora
  • Similarities with certain trends observed in English
  • Relevance of the period variable: influenced by genre?
